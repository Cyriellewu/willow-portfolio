ACML 2025 / PMLR 304 / First author

The model was not the first problem.

First-author research in a four-author collaboration. The work began with an industrial prediction request, then became a story about problem definition, usable labels, representation design, and evaluation that could survive leakage.

01 / The first problem

The initial data could not reliably answer the initial question.

“Predict failure” sounds straightforward until the available inputs do not line up with a usable outcome. In the initial industrial data, source alignment was unreliable and missing observations were ambiguous. Before changing models, I had to ask what decision the system was meant to support.

02 / Clarify before optimizing

A maintenance-linked target changed the whole project.

After clarifying the decision to support, maintenance outcomes made three supervised tasks possible: emergency-fee existence, emergency-fee cost tier, and subsystem category. That change turned a vague failure request into targets that could be evaluated against real maintenance outcomes.

03 / Earn the graph

Representation changed before the model did.

Sparse fault-code features treated related events as independent. The representation evolved from one-hot codes to aggregated contextual features, then to weighted graphs where relationships could become first-class: events close in time, events in related contexts, and events that repeatedly co-occur.

04 / Treat evaluation as product design

Split by vehicle and time - not randomly.

The published evaluation uses vehicle-wise chronological cross-validation to reduce temporal and inter-vehicle leakage. The point was not just a strong score; it was to avoid a score that looked strong because the future had quietly leaked into the past.

05 / Published result

High-cost failure precision improved from about 60% to 95%.

On the published task formulation, the system reached 98.9% fault-category accuracy and raised high-cost failure precision from about 60% to 95%. Apriori rule mining made co-fault patterns inspectable rather than leaving the graph representation as a black box. See the publication for task-level metric definitions and evaluation context.

06 / What changed in my thinking

A usable target and leakage-resistant evaluation matter before model novelty.

This project changed how I approach applied ML: more records are not automatically more evidence, and an evaluation protocol is part of the system design rather than a final reporting step.

07 / Public boundary

Public evidence, without proprietary figures.

The research used proprietary manufacturer datasets. This case study keeps the published result and method description, but intentionally does not reproduce dataset examples, internal figures, or unpublished implementation details.
